Managing attrition using simple analytics

Tej Mehta Feb 28, 2017

We have all read about the use of advanced analytics techniques (such as machine learning) being used for predicting (and preventing) employee attrition. Machine learning predictions can be sufficiently accurate and thus very effective in enabling targeted interventions for retaining high risk employees. However, using such techniques requires significant expertise in developing predictive models and experience in interpreting the outputs.

HR leaders and aspiring analysts neednâ€™t be disheartened though. One can start with some very simple analyses using nothing more than basic Excel and develop reasonably good retention strategies. I have outlined one such approach below. Obviously HR practitioners must consider the relevant business context and data availability before implementing it.

  • 1. Select one employee group

Choose a group of employees that has high levels of attrition. Ensure this group is from a single functional area, and the employees within the group have similar roles. The size of the group should be large enough to give us statistically significant results - usually 500 or more should suffice.

  • 2. Develop key hypotheses

Work with relevant business partners and other HR colleagues to identify the key factors leading to attrition. Create a laundry list of these factors (e.g. supervisor, market factors, child care facilities in the vicinity etc.). Do not use any expert judgment just yet on whether a suggested factor might indeed be a driver of attrition.

  • 3. Identify proxies and collect relevant data

From the list of hypotheses, identify those factors that have data readily available (e.g. name of supervisor), and those that need a proxy (need for child care facilities could be determined by age of employee, number of dependents, age of dependents etc.). Collect relevant data.

  • 4. Visually explore the data

Structure the data such that the output variable is always attrition (Y-axis). On X-axis plot each of the factors as input variables individually. Visually explore patterns and determine which of the hypotheses listed in step 2 hold true. Keep these variables and discard the rest.

  • 5. Establish thresholds

Based on data patterns and business knowledge, establish thresholds for each variable that would categorize a candidate into a high, medium or low risk. As an example, Tenure of 0-12 months may be high risk, 12-36 months low risk, and >36 months medium risk.

  • 6. Develop stop light risk chart

List all employees of subject group in one column and the values of all the relevant variables next to them. Create a column for overall flight risk based on weighted average of each of the variables. Color code each of the variables, and overall risk, based on the values and relevant thresholds. You can also use the attached template as a starting point.

Check with relevant stakeholders if output makes sense, and update as necessary. You can now begin to use this chart to identify individuals at high risk of attrition. Bring this to the notice of relevant line managers and business partners to take preventative action. Refine and update framework on an ongoing basis.
